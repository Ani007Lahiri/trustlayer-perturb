A pLDDT for perturbation biology

A calibrated trust layer that tells a lab which perturbation predictions to act on — and which to withhold. It doesn't try to be a better predictor. It puts an honest confidence number on any predictor.

The problem

A wrong prediction costs a lab a year.

Perturbation models (which gene knockout does what) are improving fast — but they output a number with no honest sense of when to believe it. A lab planning 40 expensive CRISPR experiments from 200 candidates has no principled way to know which predictions are trustworthy.

Structural biology got pLDDT — a per-residue confidence score shipped with every AlphaFold prediction that made the outputs usable by non-experts. Perturbation biology has no equivalent. That absence is the lane.

The idea, in one line

Wrap any predictor in a gate that must earn the right to say "go".

The gate is default-deny: a prediction is withheld unless it clears every check — genetic support, cell-type-matched eQTL evidence, a calibrated trust score, and a leakage audit. The output isn't "here's my guess" — it's commit, verify, or abstain, each with a receipt.

Try it — the gate on real genes

Type or pick a gene. The gate runs its real checks on committed data and returns a decision + receipt. Watch the largest-effect gene get withheld — the gate judges evidence, not effect size.

Does the confidence mean anything?

Yes — and it's checkable.

Under conformal calibration, when the gate says "90% coverage" it means it: on held-out data, 90% of true values fall in the predicted interval. This is the property no perturbation benchmark reports. Drag the reliability diagram below.

The test that could have killed it

We froze the T1D calibration and shipped it to a completely different assay.

If a trust layer only works on the data it was built on, it's worthless. So we took the frozen T1D gate and applied it, unchanged, to an external K562 dataset. It collapsed — coverage fell to 0.465. We report this honestly; it's the setup for the real result.

0.465frozen cross-assay coverage — a genuine null

The recovery

Recalibrate on ~1000 cells of the new assay → 0.709.

Pre-registered (hash-frozen before running), permutation p≈0.0005, survives depth controls and leave-one-donor-out. The honest claim: the method doesn't transfer frozen across assays — but it recovers with a small recalibration set, which is exactly how a lab would deploy it.

The benchmark — wraps any predictor, across contexts

The claim "it wraps any predictor" is now runnable, not narrative: 5 predictors × 3 independent public datasets. The primary axis is calibration validity + selective prediction — the axis OP3, PerturBench, VCBench and the Arc Virtual Cell Challenge don't report.

Top row: the gate delivers valid coverage across predictors and contexts — and catches RandomForest breaking at small n (twice). Bottom: a GEARS-style relational predictor is the strongest real learner on the T-cell data (R²=0.39) — and the gate calibrates it too.

An adversarial honesty engine

Every result on the previous slides survived a Claude council told to be brutal — and several earlier claims didn't, and were retracted. That's the point.

Retracted: a "0.829" drug-target win that turned out to be a hand-built surrogate, not the committed gate.
Removed pre-run: a feature giving a spurious R²=0.92 — it was a construction artifact of the target, not signal.
Reported as null: the frozen cross-assay collapse to 0.465, rather than buried.
Held honest: "GEARS-style," never "GEARS" — no GNN was trained, and we say so.

The scale is tiered by kind of evidence, not linear. Moving past ~78 needs either a genuinely novel algorithm (the 80 wall) or wet-lab validation (the 90 wall) — both named, neither faked. On a normal hackathon rubric this same work scores far higher; the Nobel anchor makes 77 read modest on purpose.

what this is
